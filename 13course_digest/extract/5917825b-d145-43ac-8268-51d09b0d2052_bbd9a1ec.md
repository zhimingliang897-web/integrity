## Motivation of PACF

• Sample ACF  $ \{r_{k}\} $  measures the overall linear dependence between  $ X_{t} $  and  $ X_{t-k} $ .

For many models (e.g. AR),  $ X_{t} $  can be correlated with  $ X_{t-k} $  indirectly through intermediate lags  $ X_{t-1}, \ldots, X_{t-k+1} $ .

• Partial Autocorrelation Function (PACF) aims to measure the direct linear relationship between  $ X_{t} $  and  $ X_{t-k} $  after removing the effect of  $ X_{t-1}, \ldots, X_{t-k+1} $ .

## AR(1) Intuition: $X_{t} = \phi X_{t-1} + Z_{t}$ with $Z_{t} \sim \text{WN}(0, \sigma^{2})$

 $ X_{t} $  and  $ X_{t-2} $  are correlated mainly via  $ X_{t-1} $ . PACF at lag 2 removes this chain effect.

To this end, we introduce the concept of best linear predictor:

## Definition (Best Linear Predictor)

For any  $ \tau $ ,  $ t \leq s $ , let  $ \widehat{X}_{\tau}^{[t:s]} $  be the best linear predictor of  $ X_{\tau} $  based on  $ (X_{t},\ldots,X_{s}) $ :

 $$ \widehat{X}_{\tau}^{[t:s]}=\beta_{1}X_{t}+\cdots+\beta_{s-t+1}X_{s}, $$ 

where  $ (\beta_{1},\ldots,\beta_{s-t+1}) $  minimizes  $ \mathbb{E}\left[X_{\tau}-\beta_{1}X_{t}-\cdots-\beta_{s-t+1}X_{s}\right]^{2} $

## PACF via Linear Prediction and its Regression Interpretation

## Definition (Partial Autocorrelation Function (PACF))

For a weakly stationary process $\{X_{t}\}$ with ACF $\{p_{k}\}$, define

 $$ \phi_{11}=\operatorname{Corr}(X_{t+1},X_{t})=\rho_{1}, $$ 

and for  $ k \geq 2 $ ,

 $$ \phi_{k k}=\operatorname{C o r r}(X_{t+k}-\widehat{X}_{t+k}^{[(t+1):(t+k-1)]} $$ 

 $$ X_{t}-\widehat{X}_{t}^{[(t+1):(t+k-1)]}) $$ 

i.e. the correlation between  $ X_{t+k} $  and  $ X_{t} $  after removing their linear dependence on  $ X_{t+1}, \ldots, X_{t+k-1} $ .

A convenient equivalent definition is via regression.

## Definition (PACF as a Regression Coefficient)

For each  $ k \geq 1 $ , consider the linear regression

## t→t+k

 $$ X_{t}=\alpha_{k,1}X_{t-1}+\alpha_{k,2}X_{t-2}+\cdots+\alpha_{k,k}X_{t-k}+\mathsf{e}_{t,k}, $$ 

where the coefficients minimize  $ E[e_{t,k}^{2}] $ . Then the PACF at lag k is  $ \phi_{kk} = \hat{\alpha}_{k,k} $ .  $ \phi_{kk} $  measures the marginal contribution of  $ X_{t-k} $  after accounting for  $ X_{t-1}, \ldots, X_{t-k+1} $ .

Partial Autocorrelation Function (PACF)

## Computing  $ \phi_{kk} $ : Yule–Walker System

Let  $ \gamma_{k}=\mathrm{Cov}(X_{t},X_{t-k}) $  and  $ \rho_{k}=\gamma_{k}/\gamma_{0} $ 

For a fixed k, the optimal regression coefficients  $ \phi_{k} = (\phi_{k1}, \ldots, \phi_{kk})^{\top} $  satisfy the Yule–Walker equations:

 $$ \Gamma_{k}\phi_{k}=\rho_{k}, $$ 

where

 $$ \boldsymbol{\Gamma}_{k}=\begin{pmatrix}1&\rho_{1}&\rho_{2}&\cdots&\rho_{k-1}\\\rho_{1}&1&\rho_{1}&\cdots&\rho_{k-2}\\\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\ddots&\vdots\\\rho_{k-1}&\rho_{k-2}&\rho_{k-3}&\cdots&1\end{pmatrix},\qquad\boldsymbol{\rho}_{k}=\begin{pmatrix}\rho_{1}\\\rho_{2}\\\vdots\\\rho_{k}\end{pmatrix}. $$ 

## PACF is the Last Component

 $$ \phi_{k k}=\left(\Gamma_{k}^{-1}\pmb{\rho}_{k}\right)_{k}. $$

Partial Autocorrelation Function (PACF)

## Closed Forms for Small Lags

Example (Lag 1)

 $$ \phi_{11}=\rho_{1}. $$ 

## Example (Lag 2)

 $$ \begin{pmatrix}1&\rho_{1}\\\rho_{1}&1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}\phi_{21}\\\phi_{22}\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}\rho_{1}\\\rho_{2}\end{pmatrix}\quad\Rightarrow\quad\phi_{22}=\frac{\rho_{2}-\rho_{1}^{2}}{1-\rho_{1}^{2}}\left(=\phi_{2}\right). $$ 

## Theorem (Cutoff for AR(p))

## I f  $ \{X_{t}\} $  follows an AR(p) model

 $$ \phi(B)(X_{t}-\mu)=Z_{t}, $$ 

 $$ Z_{t}\sim W N(0,\sigma^{2}), $$ 

then

 $$ \phi_{k k}=0,\qquad k>p, $$ 

and  $ \phi_{pp} $  equals the last AR coefficient in the AR(p) representation.

## Sample PACF: Durbin–Levinson Recursion

## General Comment

For larger k, direct inversion is possible but inefficient; we use recursion (Durbin–Levinson) in practice.

Given data  $ X_{1},\ldots,X_{N} $ , compute sample ACF  $ r_{k} $ . Define the sample PACF recursively:

 $$ \hat{\phi}_{11}=r_{1}, $$ 

and for  $ k \geq 2 $ ,

 $$ \hat{\phi}_{k k}=\frac{r_{k}-\sum_{j=1}^{k-1}\hat{\phi}_{k-1,j}r_{k-j}}{1-\sum_{j=1}^{k-1}\hat{\phi}_{k-1,j}r_{j}}. $$ 

The remaining coefficients update by

 $$ \hat{\phi}_{k,j}=\hat{\phi}_{k-1,j}-\hat{\phi}_{k k}\hat{\phi}_{k-1,k-j},\qquad j=1,\ldots,k-1. $$ 

## Practical note

This produces  $ \hat{\phi}_{kk} $  efficiently for  $ k = 1, 2, \ldots, K $  without matrix inversions.

## Significance of Sample PACF Spikes (and Sample ACF Spikes)

For identification, we need a rough rule for whether  $ \hat{\phi}_{kk} $  is significantly nonzero.

## Theorem (Large-Sample Rule of Thumb)

If the underlying process is  $ AR(p) $ , then for  $ k \geq p + 1 $ ,

 $$ \hat{\phi}_{k k}\stackrel{d}{\approx}\mathcal{N}\Big(0,\frac{1}{N}\Big), $$ 

 $$ \begin{array}{r l}{s o}&{{}\sqrt{N}\;\hat{\phi}_{k k}\stackrel{d}{\approx}\mathcal{N}(0,1).}\end{array} $$ 

## Approximate 95% Bound of Sample PACF

 $$ |\hat{\phi}_{k k}|\leq\frac{1.96}{\sqrt{N}}\quad\Rightarrow\quad\text{“not significant”(approx.)}. $$ 

For comparison, we have similar results for sample ACF:

## Approximate 95% Bound of Sample ACF

 $$ |r_{k}|\leq\frac{1.96}{\sqrt{N}}\quad\Rightarrow\quad\text{“not significant”(approx.)}. $$

Partial Autocorrelation Function (PACF)

## Example: MA(1) PACF Decays (Not Cutoff)

Let  $ X_{t}=Z_{t}+\theta Z_{t-1} $  with  $ Z_{t}\sim\text{WN}(0,\sigma^{2}) $ . Then the ACF cuts off after lag 1:

 $$ \rho_{1}=\frac{\theta}{1+\theta^{2}},\qquad\rho_{k}=0\;(k>1). $$ 

However, the PACF does not cut off; it decays in magnitude.

## Typical Behavior

For MA(1),  $ \phi_{kk} $  is dominated by a damped exponential in k, and may alternate in sign depending on the sign of  $ \theta $  (equivalently the sign of  $ \rho_{1} $ ).

• This is why PACF is most diagnostic for identifying AR order.

For MA order, the ACF cutoff is the key feature.

## Box–Jenkins Identification Strategy

The classical Box–Jenkins approach proceeds iteratively:

① Check stationarity (visual inspection, differencing if necessary).

② Examine the sample ACF  $ \{r_{k}\} $  and PACF  $ \{\hat{\phi}_{kk}\} $ .

3 Propose a tentative ARMA $ (p, q) $  model.

4 Estimate parameters.

⑦ Diagnose residuals and revise if needed.

Given an observed time series  $ \{X_{t}\} $ , determine a tentative stochastic model structure.

• Focus on identifying:

AR order p, MA order q, (and differencing if needed).

## Philosophy

Model identification is not exact; it is a data-driven, heuristic step guided by second-order statistical properties.

## ACF and PACF Patterns for AR, MA, ARMA

• Tails off: exponentially or damped oscillations

• AR(p): φ(B)(Xt − µ) = Zt.

MA(q):  $ X_{t} = \theta(B)Z_{t} $ .

• ARMA $ (p,q) $ :  $ \phi(B)(X_{t}-\mu)=\theta(B)Z_{t} $

## I nterpreting Tail Behavior

The shape of decay carries information about model roots.

• Real roots  $ \Rightarrow $  monotone exponential decay.

• Complex conjugate roots  $ \Rightarrow $  damped oscillations.

## AR polynomial

Let

 $$ \phi(z)=1-\phi_{1}z-\cdots-\phi_{p}z^{p}=\prod_{j=1}^{p}(1-\xi_{j}z). $$ 

If  $ |\xi_{j}| < 1 $ , then the ACF behaves like a linear combination of

 $$ |\xi_{j}|^{k}\cos(k\omega_{j}+\delta_{j}). $$ 

• Similar interpretations apply to PACF behavior.

## Near-Cancellation and Identification Difficulty

![](extract/viewrange_chunk_3_11_15_08bbcafd/images/page_2_img_in_image_box_658_211_729_312.jpg)

 $$ \vert-\phi B=140B, $$ 

 $$ (x_{t}-M)=z_{t} $$ 

 $$ x_{t}=\mu+7t $$ 

Consider ARMA(1, 1):

 $$ (1-\phi B)(X_{t}-\mu)=(1+\theta B)Z_{t}. $$ 

## Near cancellation

If $\phi\approx-\theta$, then

 $$ \frac{1+\theta B}{1-\phi B}\approx1, $$ 

and the process resembles white noise.

• ACF and PACF may show weak or misleading patterns.

• Identification becomes unreliable based on second-order properties alone.
